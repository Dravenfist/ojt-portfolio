Section 03

Background of Training & Project Involvement

The projects we were involved in, their objectives, and our specific responsibilities.

01
GenSpace — Appointment Management System

GenSpace is a MERN-stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React.js, Node.js) appointment management system designed to facilitate the scheduling and management of rooms and meetings for service providers. The project was inherited from a previous development team with significant technical debt — including broken imports, missing files, mismatched frontend-backend logic, and a hybrid Firebase-MongoDB database architecture. Our team took ownership of stabilizing, rebuilding, and extending the system, with John Patrick handling frontend and backend development, and Roi Jefferson managing quality assurance and bug tracking.

MERN Stack Development Firebase Authentication QA & Bug Tracking Database Migration
02
Momentum — Project Management Dashboard

Momentum is a full-featured project management application with multiple dashboard visualization tabs including Kanban, Gantt, Calendar, Chart, and TasksOverview. Scott was assigned as the primary frontend and backend developer, responsible for rewriting all major dashboard modules, implementing workspace management with a WorkspaceContext architecture, and securing the admin login flow after discovering a critical plaintext password vulnerability. The project was built with React.js and PHP, following REST API design conventions and Agile development practices with daily stand-up meetings.

React.js & PHP Dashboard Visualization Security Hardening REST API Design Workspace Management
03
ChainLogic — Inventory & Category Management System

ChainLogic is an inventory management system tracking raw materials, categories, and expiration dates. Lyka Joyce Arnesto (QA) was assigned to test the Category Library module (delete button functionality, PDF and Excel exports, checkbox selection for all rows, missing active/inactive status option) and the Expired Raw Materials module (filters, sorting, Shelf Life Date column validation). She verified proper parent-child category structure, integration with Inventory, and performed regression testing after fixes were deployed. Bugs were tracked through Jira.

Functional Testing Boundary Value Analysis Jira Bug Tracking CSV/PDF Export Validation Regression Testing
04
HirayaPay — Financial Management with OCR Receipt Scanner

HirayaPay is a financial PWA/Android APK with OCR receipt scanning (Groq Vision + Tesseract fallback). Dhan Angelo Bernardo validated Row-Level Security (RLS) policies, UI performance on mobile devices (animation lag profiling), and security hardening (input validation, rate limiting). Marjorie Orenia performed regression testing on authentication, transaction modules, wallet balance updates, and OCR accuracy, comparing the Groq Vision pipeline against the Tesseract fallback. Angel Mae Pacquing tested cross-platform authentication (Google OAuth on web, Android APK, iOS PWA) and CSV export functionality across browsers, documenting iOS-specific limitations.

RLS & Data Security OCR Validation (Groq/Tesseract) iOS PWA / Android APK Supabase Backend Linear Issue Tracking
05
SMM & Graphics — Brand Communications & Event Coverage

Maric Bolonos and Vanesa Pana supported Gencys’ Social Media and Graphics team, creating promotional materials, holiday pubmats (Independence Day, Heroes Day, Holy Week, Labor Day, etc.), event coverage for Hackathon 2026, and logo design for Valenin. Responsibilities included video editing of interview clips, managing the GDrive asset repository, designing Zoom meeting templates, and ensuring brand consistency across Facebook and internal channels. Vanesa also initiated a full reorganization of the SMM GDrive folder, greatly improving file accessibility.

Canva / Photoshop Video Editing Brand Identity (Valenin) Content Strategy Hackathon Documentation

"The internship at Gencys was formative not because everything went smoothly, but precisely because it did not. Real software development is messy, and navigating that mess taught us more than any well-structured academic project could."

Our OJT experience at Gencys gave us direct exposure to how software is developed in a commercial setting, far beyond what we experienced in classroom exercises. We learned to work with inherited codebases laden with technical debt, handle real-world bug tracking workflows, and communicate progress professionally through daily stand-ups and structured reports.

The team dynamics were eye-opening. We learned that QA is not just "finding bugs" but providing actionable information that helps developers fix issues efficiently. A vague bug report wastes everyone's time; a clear, reproducible bug report saves hours. We also learned that security vulnerabilities (like the plaintext password issue Scott discovered) require immediate action and professional responsibility.

This experience reinforced that "works on my machine" is never a valid quality standard. Cross-platform testing on actual devices (Android APK, iOS PWA, multiple browsers) is essential for production-ready applications. We will carry this discipline forward into our careers, always testing on the platforms our users actually use.

Section 07

Discipline-Related Solutions Integrated

How our academic training contributed real solutions to the company's challenges.

Challenge Identified
Legacy Codebase with Technical Debt (GenSpace)

The GenSpace codebase was inherited with missing files, broken imports, mismatched frontend-backend logic, and a hybrid Firebase-MongoDB architecture causing authentication failures. John Patrick Rolloda applied software engineering principles of separation of concerns and database normalization to consolidate the database entirely into Firebase, tracing and relinking all affected modules. He systematically rebuilt the login, register, and forgot-password flows, restoring full authentication functionality that had been broken for months.

Challenge Identified
Critical Security Vulnerability (Momentum)

During regression testing, Scott Mamhut discovered that passwords were being stored in plaintext in the admin login flow due to a type mismatch where validation expected an integer instead of a string. Applying principles of authentication theory and the principle of least privilege, he fixed the vulnerability immediately before pushing to the remote repository, removed password logging from production, and improved login error UI to avoid user enumeration vulnerabilities.

Challenge Identified
Data Privacy & Isolation (HirayaPay)

The HirayaPay application handles users' financial data including transactions, wallet balances, and savings goals. Dhan Angelo Bernardo applied Row-Level Security (RLS) testing principles to validate that users could only see their own data. He created multiple test accounts and verified cross-account isolation, ensuring compliance with data privacy regulations and preventing unauthorized data access.

Challenge Identified
Cross-Platform Inconsistencies (HirayaPay)

Google Authentication worked correctly on web and Android but failed on iOS PWA due to differences in Safari's OAuth redirect handling. Angel Mae Pacquing applied cross-platform testing theory and equivalence partitioning to systematically document the issue and provide developers with specific reproduction steps, enabling informed decisions about fixes or documented limitations for users.

Challenge Identified
Disorganized Digital Asset Repository (SMM)

The SMM team's GDrive repository had no consistent folder structure, making file retrieval time-consuming and error-prone. Vanesa Joy Pana applied information architecture principles and took initiative to sort the entire repository, creating logical folder hierarchies that matched how team members searched for files, significantly improving team efficiency and reducing the risk of using outdated assets.

Section 08

Summary of OJT Reflection

Looking back on everything — our challenges, growth, and the road ahead.

Biggest Challenges

The most significant challenge was adapting to a professional codebase that was significantly larger and more complex than anything we had worked with in school. The GenSpace project had inherited technical debt — missing files, broken imports, and an inconsistent database architecture — requiring systematic analysis before any fix could be applied. Merge conflicts between feature branches (MOM-12 and MOM-15-16) also tested our version control skills. For QA members, the absence of a live testing site was a recurring blocker that limited testing coverage. For SMM members, organizing dispersed assets and meeting tight holiday posting schedules required discipline and initiative.

Proudest Successes

John Patrick successfully rebuilding the entire authentication system from scratch, restoring functionality that had been broken for months. Scott discovering and fixing the plaintext password vulnerability within the same session, preventing a potential security breach. Lyka Joyce's thorough testing of ChainLogic's Category Library and Expired Raw Materials modules, identifying critical bugs in delete, export, and filtering functionality. The QA team's comprehensive cross-platform testing of HirayaPay, documenting iOS PWA limitations. Maric and Vanesa's holiday pubmat series and Hackathon 2026 media coverage. Vanesa's proactive GDrive reorganization, an unassigned task that improved the entire SMM team's workflow.

Personal Growth

Each of us grew in different ways. John Patrick learned that systematic analysis before action is more valuable than rushing to code. Scott learned that security vulnerabilities can hide in plain sight and must be fixed immediately, not reported and left for later. Roi discovered that QA is not just testing but providing actionable information — a bug that cannot be clearly described cannot be reliably fixed. Lyka Joyce learned that patience and proactiveness are essential; she used idle time to learn Jira and explore systems rather than waiting passively. Dhan Angelo and Marjorie developed structured testing methodologies that they will carry into their careers. Angel Mae learned that "works on my machine" is never a valid quality standard. Maric learned that design is fundamentally about communication, not aesthetics alone. Vanesa learned that organization is a professional skill and that taking initiative without being assigned is valued.

Key Takeaways for Our Future

First, systematic analysis before action. In school, problems are well-defined; in the real world, the first and most important skill is understanding what is broken and why. Second, clear communication is a technical skill — a vague bug report wastes time, a reproducible one saves hours. Third, security is everyone's responsibility; always question assumptions about data handling, even in code you did not write. Fourth, cross-platform testing is not optional — users will access your application on every device imaginable. Fifth, initiative matters — Vanesa's GDrive reorganization was unassigned but improved the entire team's workflow. We will carry these lessons into our remaining academic years and our future careers, approaching every project with greater discipline, humility, and attention to quality.

The internship at Gencys was genuinely formative not because everything went smoothly, but precisely because it did not. Walking into a codebase that was partially broken, architecturally inconsistent, and left incomplete by a previous team is exactly the kind of challenge that real software development presents. Navigating it taught us more about the craft of software engineering than any well-structured academic project could. —OJT Group IT42S1, Technological Institute of the Philippines, April 2026
